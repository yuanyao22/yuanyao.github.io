## Abstract

This study focuses on identifying specific ambient temperature thresholds (cutoff values) that trigger significant changes in the gene expression (transcriptome) of elderly residents in Beijing. 

## Key Findings

* **Transcriptomic Response:** The study utilizes RNA sequencing to observe how environmental temperature fluctuations affect biological pathways in the elderly, a population highly vulnerable to climate change.
* **Cutoff Values:** It identifies specific "high-impact" temperature points (both heat and cold) where the human body's molecular regulation shifts significantly, potentially leading to increased health risks or systemic stress.
* **Health Implications:** The research links these transcriptomic changes to physiological stress, inflammation, and cardiovascular/respiratory vulnerability, providing a molecular basis for temperature-related mortality and morbidity statistics in urban environments like Beijing.
* **Policy Relevance:** The results are intended to help public health officials refine "early warning" systems for extreme weather by basing them on biological impact thresholds rather than just historical meteorological data.
